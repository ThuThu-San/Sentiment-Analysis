Sentiment Analysis of UK Development Budget Cuts in YouTube News Content

Total word counts - 1555 words

Introduction

Across the world, many major countries have reduced their spending on development and humanitarian aid. Political priorities have shifted, with leaders placing greater emphasis on national security and domestic concerns. These changes have had negative consequences for vulnerable populations, especially in conflict-affected regions. This study analyses how UK development budget cuts are portrayed on YouTube by applying sentiment analysis to news content.

People are spending more time on their mobiles and tablets and YouTube has become a highly influential platform for shaping public understanding of international development issues. However, little is known about how different types of YouTube news channels frame the UKs development budget cuts. It is unclear whether global (non-UK) channels and UK-based channels present the issue with different sentiment, levels of emphasis, or narrative framing. Although public interest in development spending appears to be increasing on YouTube, this trend has not yet been examined systematically.

Therefore, this study aims to analyse how YouTube news channelsboth UK-based and internationalexpress sentiment and frame narratives related to UK development budget cuts using data-driven analytical methods.

Data Collection

YouTube API was used to collect video information and engagement metrics from six different Youtube news channels. Maher, B. (2025) identifies the largest news publishers on YouTube at PressGazette. During the pilot test, DW, The Sun, CNN and ABC news channels were included. They were removed after the test as they did not present much on the reseach topic but ended up consuming API daily quota, causing delays and making it harder to obtain a complete dataset without noise.

Data collection was challenging due to the time constraint and YouTube APIs daily quota limits. Although the API is free to use, it allows only 10,000 units per day, which became the main limitation during data collection (Google for Developers, 2025).

Time Frame

The UK government (2020) announced that aid spending would be reduced from 0.7% to 0.5% of gross national income (GNI). This was described as a temporary decision made because of the economic impact of the Covid-19 pandemic. However, the government now does not expect to return to the 0.7% target until at least 2029. Later, in February 2025, it was announced that aid spending will be cut further to 0.3% of GNI from 2027, with the savings redirected towards higher defence spending.

In the study, only the years, 2020 and 2025, were initially selected for data collection. However, after analysing the 2020 videos, most were focused on COVID-19, which introduced noises and left very little relevant data for meaningful analysis. To improve the dataset, two additional years,2021 and 2022, were included. Due to YouTube API limitations and the scope of the project, the years 2023 and 2024 were not added, as there were no major announcements related to UK aid budget cuts during those periods.

def search_videos(channel_id, query, start_date, end_date):
    request = youtube.search().list(
        part="snippet",
        q=query,
        channelId=channel_id,
        type="video",
        maxResults=50,
        publishedAfter=start_date,
        publishedBefore=end_date
    )
    return request.execute()

def get_video_stats(video_ids):
    """Returns full video data including statistics, descriptions and tags."""
    ids = ",".join(video_ids)
    request = youtube.videos().list(
        part="snippet,statistics,contentDetails",
        id=ids
    )
    return request.execute()

Data Cleaning

There are two main steps in cleaning the data for this project. The original raw dataset has 1045 rows. First, missing values are checked and removed any duplicate entries saved as uk_aid_youtube_data_full_clean.xlsx file which contains 904 rows. Then the dataset is downloaded to review the content more closely. After examining the excel file, keywords are used to remove noises and filter out videos that are not related to the research topic and saved as uk_aid_youtube_data_full_clean_noise.xlsx file with 353 rows. The file is checked again to make sure the remaining data was relevant. However, the dataset still contained some unrelated entries.

As a final step, the excel file is manually reviewed and removed any rows that were not relevant to the study. The final cleaned dataset ready to analyse has 76 rows.

import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns

def plot_top_videos(df_youtube, top_n=20):

    #Sort by views descending
    top_df = df_youtube.sort_values("views", ascending=False).head(top_n)

    #Create a label combining channel + year
    top_df["label"] = top_df["channel_name"] + " (" + top_df["year"].astype(str) + ")"

    #Plot
    plt.figure(figsize=(12, 6))
    ax = sns.barplot(
        data=top_df,
        y="title",
        x="views",
        hue="label",
        dodge=False
    )

    #Add value labels on bars
    for i, v in enumerate(top_df["views"]):
        ax.text(
            v + (v * 0.01),   # small offset to the right of bar
            i,                # row index
            f"{v:,}",         # add commas
            va="center"
        )
        
    plt.title(f"Top {top_n} most viewed videos (News Channels & Year)")
    plt.suptitle("Figure 1: Top 20 most viewed Youtube videos (News channels and years)", y=0.02, fontsize=12)
    plt.xlabel("Video views")
    plt.ylabel("Video titles")
    plt.legend(title="Channel (Year)")
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.show()
plot_top_videos(df_youtube, top_n=20)

Figure 1 shows the top 20 most viewed YouTube videos per news channel for each year. The bar chart provides a clear overview of which channels received the most views during the selected timeframe. SKY has the highest-viewed videos in 2022, followed by BBC News in 2020 and 2025.

It is interesting to note that the top three video titles do not directly mention UK aid budget cuts, while most of the remaining titles in the top 20 list include keywords such as UK foreign aid budget, UK foreign aid cuts, cutting foreign aid budget, and DFID and FCO merger.

sia = SentimentIntensityAnalyzer()
#Build sentiment dictionary
sentiment_rows = []

for i, row in tqdm(df_youtube.iterrows(), total=len(df_youtube)):
    video_id = row["video_id"]
    title = str(row["title"])
    desc  = str(row["description"])

    title_scores = sia.polarity_scores(title)
    desc_scores  = sia.polarity_scores(desc)

    sentiment_rows.append({
        "video_id": video_id,
        "title_negative": title_scores["neg"],
        "title_neutral": title_scores["neu"],
        "title_positive": title_scores["pos"],
        "title_compound": title_scores["compound"],
        "desc_negative": desc_scores["neg"],
        "desc_neutral": desc_scores["neu"],
        "desc_positive": desc_scores["pos"],
        "desc_compound": desc_scores["compound"],
    })

#Convert to DataFrame
df_sentiment = pd.DataFrame(sentiment_rows)

#Merge with the original dataframe
df_yt_sentiment = df_youtube.merge(df_sentiment, on="video_id", how="left")

#Add sentiment labels
def label_sentiment(score):
    if score >= 0.05:
        return "Positive"
    elif score <= -0.05:
        return "Negative"
    else:
        return "Neutral"

df_yt_sentiment["title_label"] = df_yt_sentiment["title_compound"].apply(label_sentiment)
df_yt_sentiment["description_label"] = df_yt_sentiment["desc_compound"].apply(label_sentiment)

Conclusion
This study looks at how YouTube news channels talk about the UK development budget cuts in 2020, 2021, 2022, and 2025. The UK has slowly reduced its spending on development and humanitarian aid, and the media is important because it helps the public understand what is happening. Among the six Youtube news channels, UK based SKY News and Al Jazeera from Qatar mostly portray the budget cuts in a negative or worrying way. The other channels usually present in more neutral and balanced tone. Curtis (2021) argues that Sky News often supports the message of the UK defence and foreign ministries, as reported by Declassified UK.


There are some challenges in this study, such as the YouTube API daily quota and the limited scope of the dataset. Future research could be improved by including YouTube comments or news articles with richer content, a wider timeframe, and more targeted keywords. Furthermore, advanced language models, such as emotion detection, could be used for analysing comments while topic modelling techniques could be applied to article-based content (Musi, 2025).
